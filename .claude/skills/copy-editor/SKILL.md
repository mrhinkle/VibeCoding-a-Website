# Copy Editor Skill

## What This Skill Does

Your content-auditor catches typos. Your copy-editor catches **weak sales pitches**. This skill applies proven copywriting frameworks to every page on your website, answering the one question that matters: **Is this actually persuading anyone to do anything?**

The difference: A grammar check finds "their/there/they're." A copy review finds "Your homepage headline took 12 words to say what Slack says in 3."

---

## Core Copywriting Frameworks

### AIDA (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action)
**Best for:** Landing pages, homepages, hero sections

- **Attention:** Does your headline stop scrolling? (Power words, specificity, emotional trigger)
- **Interest:** Does the subhead explain why they should care? (Benefit, not feature)
- **Desire:** Does the body copy make them want it? (Social proof, specificity, visualization)
- **Action:** Is the CTA obvious and compelling? (Clear value, low friction, one primary call)

*Example:* Instead of "Cloud Storage Solutions" → "Keep Your Files Safe Everywhere—No Lost Documents Ever Again"

### PAS (Problem, Agitate, Solution)
**Best for:** Services pages, case studies, problem-aware audiences

- **Problem:** Name the specific pain point
- **Agitate:** Show why it costs them money/time/stress (make them feel it)
- **Solution:** Present your offering as the obvious answer

*Example:* Problem: "Your team can't find files." Agitate: "That costs you 2 hours weekly." Solution: "Here's the system that lets anyone find anything in 10 seconds."

### BAB (Before, After, Bridge)
**Best for:** About pages, results-driven content, transformation stories

- **Before:** Where your audience was (struggling, frustrated, lost)
- **After:** Where they could be (confident, winning, solved)
- **Bridge:** How your product/service gets them there

*Example:* Before: "Scattered across five tools." After: "One dashboard, perfect clarity." Bridge: "That's why teams choose [Your Product]."

### 4 Ps (Promise, Picture, Proof, Push)
**Best for:** CTAs, conversion sections, high-stakes asks

- **Promise:** The specific benefit they get (not the feature, the outcome)
- **Picture:** Help them visualize success
- **Proof:** Social proof, data, testimonials, case studies
- **Push:** The CTA (with urgency or scarcity, if honest)

*Example:* Promise: "Get your first lead within 7 days." Picture: "Imagine your calendar full of qualified prospects." Proof: "3,200+ businesses have already done this." Push: "Start free today—no credit card required."

## Headline Analysis & Testing

### Scoring Rubric
Rate your headline on these 5 factors (1–5 scale):

**Emotional Appeal** (1–5)
- Curiosity: Does it make them want to know more?
- Urgency: Does it suggest time-sensitivity?
- Value: Does it promise a tangible benefit?
- Fear: Does it highlight a risk they're facing?
- Aspiration: Does it paint a desirable future?

**Power Words** (0–5 presence count)
Look for verbs that trigger action: "Discover," "Unlock," "Master," "Transform," "Guarantee," "Proven," "Secret," "The Only," "Never Again," "Guaranteed"

**Specificity** (1–5)
- Vague: "Better Solutions" (score: 1)
- Concrete: "Cut Payroll Processing Time by 73% in 30 Days" (score: 5)

**Length Optimization** (6–12 words ideal for web)
- Too short (2–3 words): "Sell Online" → misses context
- Sweet spot (6–12 words): "Sell Online Faster—The Platform 8,000+ Retailers Trust"
- Too long (15+ words): Loses readers, dilutes impact

**Readability Grade**
Use Flesch-Kincaid: Aim for 6th–8th grade level for web copy. (11th grade = too complex.)

### A/B Test Variants
Always propose 3 headline alternatives:
1. **Curiosity-driven:** "The One Thing Most Websites Get Wrong (And How to Fix It)"
2. **Benefit-driven:** "Get More Customers Without Spending More on Ads"
3. **Credibility-driven:** "Trusted by 500+ Enterprise Brands to Convert Visitors Into Buyers"

## CTA Optimization

### Action Verb Strength
Rank from weakest to strongest for web conversion:
1. "Submit" (procedural, low energy)
2. "Get" (transactional, neutral)
3. "Start" (forward-looking, slightly stronger)
4. "Discover" (curious, exploratory)
5. "Unlock" (exclusive, immediate value)
6. "Claim" (scarcity, ownership)

### Value-Forward vs. Action-Forward CTAs
- **Action-forward:** "Sign Up" (assumes they know the value)
- **Value-forward:** "Unlock Your Free 30-Day Trial" (sells the benefit)

For uncertain audiences, always be value-forward.

### Button Text Length & Specificity
- **Ideal:** 2–5 words ("Get Started Free," "Start My Audit")
- **Avoid:** Generic buttons ("Click Here," "Submit") or overly long ("Click Here to Begin Your 30-Day Free Trial") 
- **Specific > Generic:** "Schedule a Demo" beats "Let's Talk"

### Urgency & Scarcity (Use Honestly)
- Natural scarcity: "Join 3,200+ Teams Already Using [Product]"
- Real deadline: "Conference Early-Bird Price Ends Friday"
- Avoid: Fake countdown timers, misleading scarcity

### Placement Analysis
- **Primary CTA:** Above the fold, after the core value prop
- **Secondary CTAs:** End of each major section
- **Multiple touchpoints:** Footer, sticky header (mobile), exit-intent (if appropriate)
- **Friction check:** Can a first-time visitor find the CTA in under 3 seconds?

## Readability Scoring

### Grade Level (Flesch-Kincaid)
- **Below 6th grade:** Too simplistic (risks sounding patronizing)
- **6th–8th grade:** Ideal for web (wide audience comprehension)
- **9th–11th grade:** Getting dense (cut jargon, shorten sentences)
- **12th grade+:** Too academic (rewrite for clarity)

### Sentence & Paragraph Length
- **Target sentence length:** 12–15 words average
- **Paragraph length:** 2–4 sentences max (white space = readability)
- **Passive voice:** Under 10% (check: "is made by" = passive; "we create" = active)

### Jargon Detection
- Industry terms okay if your audience uses them daily
- Explain or cut terms that only experts know
- Ask: "Would a 16-year-old understand this?"

### The "So What?" Test
Read every paragraph. Ask: "So what? Why should they care?"
- Weak: "We use advanced technology."
- Strong: "That means your data is encrypted and safe."

## Page-by-Page Copy Strategy

### Homepage
**The 5-Second Test:** Can a new visitor understand what you do and why it matters in 5 seconds?

Check for:
- Hero headline: Clear, specific, benefit-driven
- Subheadline: Explains the "why"
- Primary CTA: Visible, compelling, specific
- Trust signals: Below the fold (logos, testimonials, metrics)

### About Page
**Story Arc:** Credibility + connection, not just credentials.

Check for:
- Origin story: Why did you start this?
- Credibility markers: Awards, experience, customer count
- Emotional connection: What do you believe? Why should people trust you?
- Call to next action: Don't end with history—pivot to "Here's how we can help you"

### Services / Products Page
**Benefits over features—always.**

Check for:
- Each section leads with outcome, not capability
- Example: "Collaborate Instantly Across Teams" (benefit) not "Real-time synchronization" (feature)
- Social proof positioned after value prop, not buried in footer
- CTA on every major section (don't force them to scroll to convert)

### Contact Page
**Reduce friction.**

Check for:
- Single, clear form (every field you ask = drop-off risk)
- Reassurance copy: "We'll respond within 2 hours" or "Your info is safe"
- Multiple contact methods: Phone, email, chat (options reduce anxiety)
- No requirement for phone if email is enough

### Footer
**Trust + navigation, not dumping ground.**

Check for:
- Secondary CTAs (book a demo, subscribe, download)
- Trust signals: Certifications, memberships, years in business
- Navigation completeness: Can users find what they need?

## Microcopy Review

These tiny moments often drive conversion more than headline copy:

- **Button text:** "Start my free trial" vs. "Get started" 
- **Form labels:** "Work email" (specific) vs. "Email" (generic)
- **Error messages:** "That email is already registered" (helpful) vs. "Error" (useless)
- **Success messages:** "Your demo is scheduled for Thursday at 2pm" (concrete) vs. "Success!" (hollow)
- **Placeholder text:** "name@company.com" (helpful) vs. blank
- **Navigation labels:** "See how it works" (action) vs. "Features" (generic)
- **404 page copy:** Don't waste this moment—use humor or a CTA

## Competitive Messaging Comparison

**Question:** If you replaced your company name with a competitor's, would anyone notice?

Check for:
- Generic phrases everyone uses ("Best-in-class solutions," "Innovative technology")
- Where you zig while they zag—what's truly different?
- Are you claiming the same benefits as 5 competitors?
- What can only you say honestly?
